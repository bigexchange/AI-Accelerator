The AI User's Field Guide

Everyone's using AI. Not everyone's using it well. This guide is about getting to the right end of that spectrum — rethinking how you approach your work, what you tackle first, and how you stop doing things the slow way out of habit.

🧭
What you'll have
A clear mental model for using AI well — not just using it more. Eight parts covering mindset, prompting, overconfidence, and advanced moves.

Everyone's using AI. Not everyone's using it well.

There's a spectrum. On one end: people who paste a vague prompt, accept the first output, and move on. On the other: people who've genuinely changed how they work — faster decisions, sharper thinking, more time on the problems that actually need a human.

This guide is about getting to the second end of that spectrum. It's not about writing better prompts for email subject lines. It's about rethinking how you approach your work — what you tackle first, where you spend your energy, and how you stop doing things the slow way out of habit.

A few things this guide is not:

  • A lecture about AI risks (you've heard it)
  • A hype piece about AI changing everything (you've heard that too)
  • A list of 47 ChatGPT hacks that stopped working six months ago

Let's get into it.

Part 1: The Mindset Shift Nobody Talks About

AI is not a vending machine. It's a challenge to your process. Most people bolt AI onto their existing workflow and wonder why the results are underwhelming. They do the same work the same way — they just use AI to speed up a step or two. The bigger unlock is asking a different question: *Why am I doing this at all?* Not "how do I do this faster," but "does this task need to work the way it's always worked?" AI is a forcing function. It compresses timelines so aggressively that it exposes every unnecessary step, every approval layer that exists out of habit, every deliverable that gets made because it's always been made — not because it moves anything. The people who get the most out of AI aren't the power users with the best prompts. They're the ones who looked at their week, figured out where their actual leverage was, and restructured everything else around it. The mindset shift: Stop asking AI to help you do your current job. Start asking AI to help you question whether you're doing the right job in the right way.

Part 2: What AI Is Actually Good For

Before tactics, a clear-eyed inventory.

AI is genuinely excellent at

  • First drafts of almost anything — so you're always reacting, never starting from zero
  • Synthesizing large amounts of information quickly
  • Generating options when you're stuck or need to pressure-test an approach
  • Breaking complex work into structured steps you can act on
  • Handling the tedious, repeatable parts of a task so you can focus on the judgment-heavy parts

AI is mediocre at

  • Knowing what you actually want without context
  • Understanding the nuance of your organization, your audience, or your relationships
  • Producing work that sounds like a specific person without being trained to
  • Making the right call in genuinely ambiguous situations

AI is bad at

  • Admitting uncertainty (more on this shortly)
  • Knowing when it doesn't know something
  • Replacing judgment, relationships, or real expertise

The biggest efficiency gains come from offloading the first category aggressively — and staying firmly in charge of the last one.

Part 3: Prompting Is a Skill — Here's How to Get Good at It

Bad prompt: "Summarize this for me."

Good prompt: "Summarize this for a VP who has three minutes and needs to make a decision. Flag any risks or open questions. Bullet points, plain language."

The difference isn't length. It's clarity about what done looks like. A strong prompt includes:

Role / Audience — Who is this for? ("Write for a non-technical executive")

Goal — What should the output accomplish? ("I need to make a decision," "I need to brief my team," "I need to respond to a client")

Format — What does done look like? ("Three options with tradeoffs," "a one-paragraph summary," "a list of questions to ask")

Constraints — What to avoid or stay within? ("Keep it under 200 words," "don't recommend a vendor yet")

Context — What does the AI need to know? ("This is for an internal audience that already knows the background," "our biggest concern is X")

More investment upfront = less revision on the back end. Shift your time from fixing outputs to writing better inputs.

And iterate. Your first prompt is a first draft. If the output isn't right, don't start over — tell it what to fix. AI conversations compound.

Part 4: AI Is the Most Overconfident Person You've Ever Met. Act Accordingly.

This is the part most guides skip.

AI does not hedge. It does not say "I'm not sure about this" unprompted. It will state something wrong with the same confident, well-structured delivery it uses to state something right — and if you're moving fast, you might not notice.

Think about the most overconfident person you've worked with. Smart, fast, always has an answer, occasionally catastrophically wrong. You learned to trust their instincts on certain things and verify them on others. You didn't take everything at face value. You pushed back.

That's the right relationship with AI.

Challenge it. If something seems off, it probably is. Ask it to walk you through its reasoning. Ask it what assumptions it made. Ask it what it's not accounting for.

Ask it to disagree with itself. "What are the strongest arguments against this?" "What could go wrong here?" "What's the version of this that doesn't work?" AI is much better at finding holes when you explicitly ask it to look for them.

Watch for false confidence in facts. Numbers, statistics, citations, names, dates — AI will generate these fluently and incorrectly. It's not lying; it's pattern-matching in a way that produces plausible-sounding output even when the underlying fact doesn't exist.

Be especially skeptical when it's especially smooth. A well-organized, clearly written answer is not the same as a correct one. The polish is the trap. The better the output looks, the more tempting it is to skip verification.

The goal isn't suspicion — it's calibration. Know where to trust it and where to push.

Part 5: You Still Have to Check the Work

This should go without saying. It often doesn't.

AI outputs are first drafts, not finished products. Treating them as finished products is where things go wrong — not just in quality, but in professional credibility.

What checking actually means Verify facts before you publish or present them. Numbers, statistics, citations, research findings — look them up. AI will produce a plausible-sounding source that doesn't exist or a statistic that's been misremembered or fabricated. One bad data point in a deck can undermine everything around it. Check that it actually answered your question. AI is very good at producing relevant-sounding content that doesn't quite address what you asked. Read it with fresh eyes: did it do the thing? Or did it do something adjacent to the thing in a way that feels close enough? Put your name on it, not AI's. When you share something — internally, externally, to a client or exec — it reflects your judgment. AI got you 70% of the way there. The remaining 30% is where your expertise, your knowledge of the room, and your credibility live. Don't skip it. Know what kind of work requires more scrutiny. Anything that involves specific facts, customer data, financial information, legal implications, or sensitive relationships needs a harder look. Fast and wrong is worse than slow and right. The efficiency gain from AI is real. It doesn't eliminate the responsibility to use your brain before hitting send.

Part 6: The AI Slop Trap

AI output left unchecked tends toward the generic. Technically correct. Completely forgettable. You know it when you see it:

  • "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..."
  • Five bullet points that all start with the same word
  • Conclusions that "underscore the importance of" whatever the post was about
  • "Leverage," "robust," "empower" as filler

This isn't a flaw in the AI — it's a regression to the mean. AI is trained to be agreeable and complete. Without guidance, it produces the most statistically average version of whatever you asked for.

The fix: give it specifics, give it constraints, give it examples of what good looks like. And edit like a human. AI builds the scaffold. You bring the voice, the edge, the actual point of view.

If you'd never say it out loud in a meeting, it shouldn't leave your hands.

Part 7: When Not to Use AI

The goal isn't to use AI as much as possible. It's to use it where it makes your work genuinely better.

Don't use AI for Judgment calls. AI can give you options, frameworks, and synthesis. It cannot tell you the right answer for your specific situation, with your specific constraints, in your specific culture. That part is yours. Anything requiring verified facts. AI will sound confident while being wrong. Verify before you publish, cite, or pitch. Sensitive communications. Difficult feedback, personnel matters, meaningful recognition — these deserve actual human thought. The friction is the point. First-person takes that need to be yours. If you have a genuine perspective, write it. Use AI to sharpen it, not replace it. The more AI-generated opinion floods every channel, the more actual human perspective becomes the differentiator.

Part 8: Advanced Moves

Once you've got the basics down, here's where the real efficiency unlocks are.

Use it to pressure test before you invest. Before you build the full deck, write the full brief, or kick off the full project — ask AI to poke holes in the approach. "What are the weakest points in this plan?" costs you five minutes and can save you two days.

Build a context block. At the start of any session, give AI a paragraph about who you are, what you're working on, and what success looks like. "I'm working on X. My audience is Y. My constraint is Z. I need..." You'll get dramatically better output with dramatically less back-and-forth.

Save what works. The prompts and frameworks that consistently produce good outputs for you are assets. Build a personal library. Reuse and refine.

Use it to get unstuck, not to avoid thinking. Ask AI for five different angles on a problem. You probably won't use any of them directly — but one will usually unlock your actual take. Let it unstick you, then take it from there.

Rebuild your workflow, not just your tools. Every few months, look at your repeating work and ask: is there a step here that exists out of habit? A format that nobody actually reads? A meeting that could be a summary? AI changes the economics of a lot of work. Give yourself permission to change the process.

The Bottom Line

AI won't replace people who think clearly and bring real expertise to their work. It will replace the habits, the busywork, and the slow parts — and raise expectations for everyone who adapts.

The practitioners who win aren't using AI the most. They're the ones who've figured out where it genuinely changes the game, where they still need to own it themselves, and how to tell the difference.

Use it like a tool. Think like a human. Check the work.
