Forecast Accuracy Tracker

Forecast accuracy is one of the most important — and most ignored — metrics in sales. Every week you call a number. Every week some of those deals close, some slip, and some die quietly. This guide shows you how to build a real accuracy picture.

🎯
What you'll have
Your actual close rate by category, win rate by segment and competitor, and a recalibrated personal benchmark that reflects how your deals actually behave.

Forecast accuracy is one of the most important — and most ignored — metrics in sales. Every week you call a number. Every week some of those deals close, some slip, and some die quietly. The question most reps and managers never actually answer is: how accurate is my forecast over time, and what does that pattern tell me about how I'm qualifying and categorizing deals?

This guide shows you how to use Claude and Salesforce to build a forecast accuracy picture — at the rep level, the team level, and the deal level — and turn it into something actionable. Because knowing your win rate is one thing. Knowing whether your Commit calls are actually closing at the rate you're claiming is a different, more useful thing entirely.

What you need before you start

  • Salesforce connected in Claude's tools panel
  • A consistent tracking habit — this guide works best when you run it weekly or at the end of each quarter
  • Optionally: a Google Sheet where you log your weekly forecast call so you can compare actuals against it over time

A note on how this works

Claude doesn't retain memory between sessions, so it can't automatically compare what you called last week against what closed this week. The tracking layer is yours — a simple Google Sheet where you log your weekly number is all you need. Claude pulls the actuals from Salesforce; you provide the called number; Claude does the comparison and analysis. Once you have a few weeks of data logged, the patterns become clear fast.

Step 1: Pull your actuals

Start with what actually happened — closed won, closed lost, and what's still open from your committed pipeline.

Prompt — this week's actualsPull my forecast actuals from Salesforce for the past 7 days. Give me: total closed won count and value, total closed lost count and value, any deals that were in my Commit or Call category at the start of the week that didn't close — list them with current stage and close date. Then give me: my actual win rate for the week by deal count and by dollar value.
Prompt — this quarter's actualsPull my closed won and closed lost opportunities from Salesforce for this fiscal quarter. Give me: total won count and value, total lost count and value, win rate by count and by dollar value, average deal size won vs. lost, and top 5 wins and top 5 losses by amount. Then give me a one-paragraph read on how the quarter is tracking.

Step 2: Compare called vs. closed

This is the core of forecast accuracy. You called a number — here's whether it materialized.

Prompt — weekly accuracy checkI called $[X] in Commit and $[Y] in Call for this week. Actual closed won was $[Z]. My forecast accuracy was [Z/X as a percentage] on Commit and [Z/(X+Y) as a percentage] on Call + Commit combined. Is this above or below a healthy forecast accuracy benchmark? What does it suggest about how I'm categorizing deals — am I over-committing, under-committing, or roughly calibrated?
Prompt — multi-week accuracy trendHere are my weekly forecast calls and actuals for the past [X] weeks: Week 1: Called $X, Closed $Y Week 2: Called $X, Closed $Y [continue] Calculate my average forecast accuracy over this period. Am I consistently over-calling, under-calling, or variable? What's the trend — am I getting more or less accurate over time? What does the pattern suggest about my forecasting habits?
Prompt — category-level accuracyOver the past quarter, here are my outcomes by forecast category: Commit deals: [X] called, [Y] closed, [Z] slipped or lost Call deals: [X] called, [Y] closed, [Z] slipped or lost Best Case deals: [X] in category, [Y] closed What is my actual close rate by forecast category? Is my Commit actually behaving like a Commit, or is it performing more like a Best Case? Help me recalibrate what each category should mean for me specifically.

Step 3: Win rate analysis

Beyond forecast accuracy, win rate tells you how well you're qualifying. BigID's current overall win rate this year is approximately 16% by deal count — meaning roughly 1 in 6 opportunities closes won. The question is whether your personal win rate is above, below, or in line with that — and what's driving the difference.

Prompt — personal win ratePull all my closed opportunities from Salesforce this year — both won and lost. Give me: total closed count, won count, lost count, win rate by count, win rate by dollar value, average deal size won, average deal size lost, and my top three loss reasons by frequency. Then tell me: where am I winning and where am I losing?
Prompt — win rate by segmentPull my closed won and closed lost opportunities from Salesforce this year. Break down my win rate by: primary use case (Security, Privacy, Governance), persona, industry, and deal size bucket. Where is my win rate strongest? Where is it weakest? What does that suggest about where I should be spending my time prospecting?
Prompt — win rate by competitorPull my closed opportunities from Salesforce this year where a competitor is logged. Break down my win rate by competitor — how often am I winning and losing against Varonis, Purview, OneTrust, Cyera, and Securit.ai? Which competitive situation is hardest for me? Which am I strongest in?

Step 4: Loss pattern analysis

This year across the org, the top loss reasons are Non Responsive (96 deals, $27M), No Budget/Lost Funding (32 deals, $9.3M), No Decision (28 deals, $7.5M), and Lack of Sponsor (20 deals, $8.9M). Non Responsive at the top is a qualification signal — deals that go non-responsive usually weren't fully qualified to begin with. Understanding your personal loss pattern tells you exactly where your process is breaking down.

Prompt — personal loss patternPull all my closed lost opportunities from Salesforce this year. Group by primary loss reason. For each reason give me: count, total lost value, and average deal size. Then tell me: what are my top three loss reasons, what stage were most of these deals in when they were lost, and what does the pattern suggest about where my sales process has the biggest leak?
Prompt — loss pattern vs. stagePull my closed lost opportunities from Salesforce this year. Show me the distribution by stage at time of loss — how many were lost at Stage 0, Stage 1, Stage 2, and so on. Where in the funnel am I losing the most deals by count? By value? Is there a specific stage where I'm losing disproportionately — and if so, what's the most common loss reason at that stage?
Prompt — non-responsive dealsPull my closed lost opportunities from Salesforce this year where the loss reason is Non Responsive. Give me: count, total value, average deal size, industry breakdown, and what stage they were in when lost. These are deals that didn't die — they just disappeared. What patterns do you see? Do the Non Responsive losses cluster in a particular industry, persona, or stage?

Step 5: Build your accuracy benchmark

Once you've tracked for a few weeks, build your personal benchmark — the close rate by category that reflects your actual history, not the theoretical one.

Based on my actual close rates over the past [X weeks/months], help me build a personal forecast model. If my Commit deals close at [X]%, my Call deals close at [Y]%, and my Best Case deals close at [Z]%, what is my realistic expected close for the quarter based on my current pipeline in each category? How much pipeline do I need in each category to hit my number?
Prompt — recalibrating your categoriesMy Commit deals are closing at [X]% historically, but I'm supposed to be calling Commit at 90%+. That means I'm over-committing. Based on my actual close rates, what should I realistically be calling Commit vs. Call vs. Best Case? Help me recalibrate my category definitions so my forecast is more accurate going forward.

The simple tracking sheet

You don't need a complex system. A Google Sheet with five columns, updated every Friday, is enough to build a meaningful accuracy picture in 4–6 weeks.

WeekCommit CalledCall CalledActual Closed WonAccuracy %
Week of [date]$X$Y$ZZ/X %

Each Friday: log your Commit and Call numbers. The following Friday: pull actuals from Salesforce and log what closed. After four weeks you have a trend. After twelve weeks you have a benchmark.

Prompt to build the sheetHelp me set up a simple forecast accuracy tracking sheet in Google Sheets. I want to track weekly: my Commit call, my Call call, actual closed won, forecast accuracy on Commit only, and forecast accuracy on Commit + Call combined. Add a row for a rolling 4-week average accuracy. Keep it simple — I want to be able to fill it in in two minutes every Friday.

Sharing accuracy data upward

Proactively sharing your forecast accuracy trend with your manager — especially when it's improving — is one of the highest-leverage things you can do for your credibility.

Prompt — manager summaryBased on my forecast accuracy data for the past [X] weeks, write a short manager update. Cover: my average forecast accuracy on Commit, whether my accuracy is improving or declining over the period, my win rate this quarter vs. last, my top loss reason and what I'm doing about it, and my realistic call for the rest of the quarter. Keep it under 150 words and make it factual — no spin.

Tips

Track called vs. closed, not just win rate. Win rate tells you how good your deals are. Forecast accuracy tells you how well you understand your deals. They measure different things and you need both.

Non Responsive losses are a qualification warning. When a deal goes non-responsive it usually means you didn't have a real champion, a real timeline, or a real compelling event. The fix isn't better follow-up — it's tighter qualification earlier. Use your Non Responsive loss data to audit your Stage 0 and Stage 1 process.

Your personal close rate by category is your real benchmark. If your Commit deals historically close at 65%, then 65% is your Commit rate — not 90%. Use your actual history to set honest expectations with your manager rather than inheriting a theoretical benchmark that doesn't reflect how your deals actually behave.

Don't clean up loss reasons retroactively. The temptation after a loss is to log something that sounds better than what actually happened. Resist it. Clean loss data is the most actionable intelligence in Salesforce — bad loss data just tells you what you wanted to believe.

Accuracy compounds. A rep who forecasts accurately at 75% is more valuable to leadership than one who swings between 40% and 120%. Consistency and predictability build trust faster than big quarters followed by misses.
