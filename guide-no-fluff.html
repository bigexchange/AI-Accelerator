No Fluff: Navigating the Blueprint for AI Tells

How to spot AI-generated content before it spots you. The em dash epidemic, the wall-of-shame verbs, and how to fix every one of them.

🔍
What you'll have
A trained eye for AI tells, a slop audit checklist to run before anything goes out the door, and a clear process for fixing what you find.

We're all using AI. And a lot of what we're producing with it sounds exactly like what it is: a machine doing its best impression of a professional.

The irony is thick. We're using AI to move faster, produce more, and free up time for higher-value work — and in the process, a lot of that output is quietly eroding the thing that made our content worth reading in the first place: a recognizable human voice with a real point of view.

This isn't an argument against using AI. It's an argument for using it better. The difference between AI content that works and AI content that gets ignored comes down to one thing: whether a human with taste actually reviewed it before it went out the door.

That requires knowing what to look for. This guide is that.

What's an AI Tell?

In poker, a tell is an unconscious habit that gives away what a player is holding. They don't know they're doing it. That's what makes it useful to everyone else.

AI has tells too. Patterns it defaults to when left to its own devices — phrasing, structure, word choices, vibes — that signal to any careful reader: a human did not agonize over this. The tell isn't any one word or phrase in isolation. It's the accumulation. It's the texture of something that was generated, not written.

Once you see them, you can't unsee them. That's the point.

The Tells

The Em Dash EpidemicAI loves an em dash — especially placed right in the middle of a sentence, just like this — as a way to add rhythm it didn't earn. One em dash in a piece of writing is a stylistic choice. Four em dashes is a tell. If your draft reads like it's constantly interrupting itself, you've found one.
The Unlock/Unleash/Elevate/Enhance ClusterThese words show up constantly in AI output because they sound active and strategic without actually saying anything. They are the verbal equivalent of a firm handshake with no follow-through. Unlocking value. Unleashing potential. Elevating your strategy. Enhancing outcomes. Read that sentence again. What did it tell you? Nothing. That's the tell. Any time you see these words, ask: what is the actual thing being described? If the sentence doesn't survive without the power verb, cut the power verb and write what you mean. The full wall of shame: navigating, unleashing, unlocking, enhancing, elevating, harnessing, transforming, empowering, enabling, reimagining, revolutionizing. "No Fluff" and Other Meta-Promises There's a particular flavor of AI output that announces its own quality before delivering it. No fluff. Straight to the point. Here's what you actually need to know. The tell isn't enthusiasm — it's that the content is describing itself instead of just being itself. Good writing doesn't need to promise it won't waste your time. It just doesn't waste your time.
The 5th Grade Soccer CoachYou know this one. It's motivational without being specific. It builds to a rallying cry that could apply to literally any situation. It ends with something like "that's the real competitive advantage" or "and that changes everything." In today's rapidly evolving landscape, organizations that embrace change will be the ones that come out ahead. The question isn't whether to adapt — it's how fast. Is that about data security? Marketing strategy? Climate change? Literally anything? Yes. That's the tell. If you could swap your company name with any other company and the content would be equally true, it isn't actually about your company.
The -ing ProblemAI has a strong gravitational pull toward gerunds and continuous tense. Navigating the complexities. Driving better outcomes. Leveraging your data. Harnessing the power of. It's not wrong, exactly. It's just soft. It makes everything feel like it's perpetually in motion without arriving anywhere. Strong writing is declarative. It says what a thing is, not what it's doing while also being. Before: Harnessing AI capabilities to drive enhanced decision-making across the enterprise. After: AI that helps your team make better decisions, faster.
Clichés That Don't Match the RoomAI doesn't know your audience. It knows language in aggregate — which means its metaphors and analogies are drawn from the broadest possible pool. The result is clichés that feel vaguely off, like a suit that's almost the right fit. Telling enterprise security buyers that your product is "a game-changer" or "moves the needle" or "separates the wheat from the chaff" isn't just bland — it signals that nobody thought about who was actually reading this. Your audience has a specific vocabulary, specific concerns, and a specific tolerance for marketing language. AI defaults to the generic. You have to override it.
Generalized Product PositioningThis one is the most dangerous, especially for companies with a specific story to tell. AI knows what it knows — which is usually a surface-level version of what your company does, stitched together from public sources of varying accuracy and freshness. Left unsupervised, it will produce positioning that is technically in the right category but subtly wrong: wrong differentiator, wrong emphasis, wrong competitive framing. For BigID specifically: we're not just another data privacy tool. We're not "a comprehensive platform for managing data risk." We're not interchangeable with Varonis or Cyera or Purview. If a piece of content could have been written about any vendor in the space, it wasn't written about us. That's a tell — and a liability. Any content that touches product positioning, competitive differentiation, or solution messaging needs human review by someone who actually knows the story. AI can draft. It cannot own the narrative.

The Slop Audit

Before anything goes out the door, run it through this checklist:

Language

  • Any em dashes that aren't earning their place?
  • Any words from the wall of shame? (unlock, unleash, elevate, enhance, harness, navigate, transform, empower)
  • Any sentences that promise no fluff, straightforward insights, or what you really need to know?
  • Any -ing phrases that could be rewritten as direct statements?

Voice and Fit

  • Does this sound like a person? Could you read it out loud without cringing?
  • Would you say any of this in a meeting?
  • Do the metaphors and analogies match the actual audience?
  • Could you swap our company name for a competitor's without changing a word?

Accuracy

  • Is the product positioning accurate — not just adjacent to accurate?
  • Are any facts, statistics, or claims verified against a real source?
  • Does this reflect how we actually talk about our product, or how a language model guessed we talk about it?

If you're checking more than a few boxes, it's not ready.

How to Fix It

Spotting the tells is half the job. Here's the other half.

Rewrite the tells, don't just delete them. Cutting a weak word usually reveals that the sentence had nothing behind it. If removing "unleashing" leaves you with nothing, the sentence needs to be rebuilt, not edited.

Put your voice in. The fastest way to kill AI slop is to write one sentence that's actually yours — specific, direct, slightly opinionated — and then let the rest of the paragraph catch up to it.

Read it out loud. Slop collapses under the weight of being spoken. If you stumble, the reader will stumble. Fix the stumble.

Give AI better instructions up front. A lot of slop is preventable. Tell AI who it's writing for, what they already know, what they're tired of hearing, and what voice to avoid. You won't eliminate the problem, but you'll reduce the cleanup.

Know when to scrap and rewrite. Sometimes a draft is so generic that editing it is slower than starting over with better inputs. Know the difference. A bad scaffold takes longer to fix than a blank page.

The Bottom Line

AI slop isn't inevitable — it's what happens when no human with taste looks at the output before it goes out. The tells exist because AI defaults to the mean. Catching them is how you make sure your work doesn't.

This isn't about being precious over writing. It's about not publishing content that signals, loudly, that you hit generate and walked away. In a world where everyone has access to the same tools, judgment and taste are the differentiators.

Use the tools. Apply the taste. Check the tells.
