Pipeline Coverage Analysis

Pipeline coverage is the ratio of open pipeline to quota. The question coverage analysis answers isn't just 'do I have enough?' — it's 'do I have the right pipeline, in the right stages, distributed across the right reps and regions, to actually hit the number?'

📐
What you'll have
A real coverage picture: weighted pipeline vs. raw, coverage ratio by rep and geo, concentration risk flagged, and a gap calculation.

Pipeline coverage is the ratio of open pipeline to quota. If you need to close $5M this quarter and you have $15M in open pipeline, your coverage ratio is 3x. The question coverage analysis answers isn't just "do I have enough?" — it's "do I have the right pipeline, in the right stages, distributed across the right reps and regions, to actually hit the number?"

A coverage ratio looks healthy until you pull it apart. $57M in pipeline sounds like plenty — until you realize $28M of it is in early discovery, $15M is owned by two reps, and half of it has a close date that already slipped once. This guide shows you how to pull the real coverage picture from Salesforce, stress-test it, and identify exactly where the gaps are before they become a quarter-end problem.

What you need before you start

  • Salesforce connected in Claude's tools panel
  • Your quota for the current quarter — at the rep, team, or org level depending on what you're analyzing
  • Optionally: last quarter's actuals to use as a close rate benchmark

How to think about coverage ratios

A coverage ratio of 3x is the standard minimum — meaning you need three dollars of pipeline for every dollar of quota. But 3x assumes a consistent close rate across all stages. In practice, early-stage pipeline closes at a much lower rate than late-stage pipeline, so the right ratio depends heavily on the mix.

A rough framework for BigID's pipeline stages:

StageExpected close rate
Stage 0–1 (Qualification, Discovery)10–20%
Stage 2 (Consensus/Demo)25–35%
Stage 3 (Proof of Value)40–55%
Stage 4 (Business Negotiation)65–75%
Stage 5 (Legal Review)80–90%

A healthy quarter has a good mix across stages — enough late-stage to feel confident about the current quarter, enough mid-stage to fill the quarter after that, and enough early-stage to know what's two quarters out.

Step 1: Pull your current coverage picture

Start with the full pipeline for your period — total pipeline, stage distribution, and the coverage ratio against your quota.

Pull all my open opportunities from Salesforce closing this quarter. Give me: total pipeline value, count of deals, and a breakdown by stage showing count and pipeline value at each stage. Then calculate my pipeline coverage ratio assuming my quota this quarter is $[X]. Tell me whether my coverage looks healthy, thin, or concentrated in a particular stage — and flag any stage where I have significantly more or less pipeline than expected. Current Q2 FY2027 baseline — for context: Right now Q2 FY2027 has 161 open deals worth approximately $57.9M across the org. Late-stage pipeline (Stage 3 and above) accounts for $28.5M of that — about 49%. North America leads at $34M, EMEAAPJ at $19M, Federal at $3M, and LATAM at $1.9M.

Step 2: Stress-test the number

Raw pipeline value is the optimistic number. Weighted pipeline — adjusted by close rate at each stage — is the realistic one. Run both.

Weighted pipeline prompt Pull my open opportunities this quarter from Salesforce broken down by stage. Apply these close rate assumptions: Stage 0–1 at 15%, Stage 2 at 30%, Stage 3 at 50%, Stage 4 at 70%, Stage 5 at 85%. Calculate my weighted pipeline value. How does it compare to my raw pipeline value? What is my weighted coverage ratio against a quota of $[X]? Is my weighted pipeline enough to hit the number, or do I have a coverage gap?
Best case vs. realistic case Based on my current open pipeline closing this quarter, give me two coverage scenarios: a best case (assuming above-average close rates) and a realistic case (assuming historical average close rates). What is the range of outcomes I should be planning for? What would I need to close from Best Case deals to hit my number if my Commit deals all close?
Late-stage concentration check Of my total open pipeline this quarter, what percentage is in Stage 3 or later? What percentage is in Stage 0–1? Is my pipeline top-heavy (too much early stage) or bottom-heavy (strong late stage but thin top of funnel)? What does this mean for this quarter vs. next quarter?

Step 3: Rep-level coverage

Overall coverage can look fine while individual reps are dangerously thin. Pull the rep-level breakdown and flag anyone who's at risk.

Pull open pipeline by rep for this quarter from Salesforce. For each rep give me: total pipeline value, deal count, breakdown by stage, and coverage ratio assuming a quota of $[X] per rep. Flag any rep whose total pipeline is below 2x quota, any rep whose pipeline is concentrated entirely in Stage 0–1, and any rep with fewer than 3 deals in their pipeline. These are coverage risks at the rep level.
Rep comparison prompt Compare my top 5 reps by pipeline value to my bottom 5 this quarter. For each group give me: average pipeline per rep, average deal count, average stage distribution, and average close rate this year based on closed won data. What separates the high-coverage reps from the low-coverage reps? Is it deal count, deal size, stage mix, or something else?

Step 4: Geo and segment coverage

Coverage gaps often cluster by region or segment. BigID's current Q2 pipeline breaks down as North America ($34M), EMEAAPJ ($19M), Federal ($3M), and LATAM ($1.9M).

Geo coverage prompt Pull my open pipeline for this quarter from Salesforce broken down by geo — North America, EMEAAPJ, Federal, and LATAM. For each geo give me: total pipeline value, deal count, and stage distribution. Assuming quota is distributed as [X% NA, Y% EMEA, Z% Federal, W% LATAM], calculate coverage ratio per geo. Which geos are over-covered? Which are at risk?
Segment coverage prompt Pull my open pipeline for this quarter from Salesforce broken down by primary use case — Security, Privacy, and Governance. For each segment give me total pipeline value, deal count, and average deal size. Is coverage evenly distributed across segments or concentrated in one? Does the distribution match where our win rates are strongest?

Step 5: Coverage gap analysis

Once you know where the gaps are, quantify them and build a creation plan.

Gap calculation prompt My current weighted pipeline for this quarter is $[X]. My quota is $[Y]. My weighted coverage ratio is [X/Y]. To reach a healthy 3x weighted coverage I would need $[3Y] in weighted pipeline — meaning I have a gap of $[3Y - X]. Based on my average deal size of $[Z] and current stage mix, how many new opportunities do I need to create this week to close that gap by end of quarter? Break it down by stage — how many Stage 1 deals, Stage 2 deals, Stage 3 deals would get me to 3x?
Next quarter coverage check Pull all my open opportunities from Salesforce closing next quarter. Give me: total pipeline value, deal count, and stage distribution. How does next quarter's pipeline look compared to this quarter at the same point? Am I building pipeline for next quarter while I close this one, or is there a cliff coming? What should I be creating now to avoid a coverage crisis next quarter?

Step 6: The coverage health scorecard

Run this at the start of every week to keep a live pulse on coverage across the team.

Give me a weekly pipeline coverage scorecard for my team. Pull from Salesforce and give me: - Total open pipeline this quarter — value and count - Weighted pipeline by stage - Coverage ratio (raw and weighted) vs. quota of $[X] - Reps below 2x coverage — names and pipeline value - Reps with no late-stage deals (Stage 3+) - New pipeline created this week vs. last week - Net pipeline change this week - One-sentence coverage health verdict — are we in good shape, at risk, or in trouble? Format it so I can paste it into a Slack message or weekly team email.

Reading the coverage signals

Coverage below 2x — you're in trouble unless your historical close rate is unusually high. You need to create pipeline now, not at end of quarter.

Coverage above 5x — either your quota is low, your pipeline is inflated with stale deals, or you're not qualifying out the things that won't close. Run a Stage 0 cleanup before you get too comfortable with the number.

All pipeline in Stage 0–1 — you'll have a good quarter in 90 days. This quarter is a problem. Focus on advancing what you have, not creating more.

All pipeline in Stage 4–5 — this quarter looks strong. Next quarter has a cliff. Start creating pipeline now.

Coverage strong overall but thin for specific reps — overall numbers hide rep-level risk. Pull the rep breakdown every week. A team with three reps at 6x and three reps at 1x doesn't have good coverage — it has concentrated risk.

Tips

Coverage ratio is a lagging indicator — act on it early. By the time your coverage drops to 1.5x it's too late to fix it through pipeline creation in the same quarter. Run the coverage analysis at the start of each week, not two weeks before quarter end.

Weighted pipeline is the honest number. Raw pipeline feels better. Weighted pipeline reflects reality. Build your plans around weighted coverage, and use raw as the upside scenario.

New pipeline created this week is your leading indicator. Coverage is a snapshot. New pipeline created is the trend. A team that creates consistent new pipeline every week maintains coverage naturally. A team that waits for big deals to come in lives quarter to quarter.

Coverage gaps by rep require different interventions. A rep with thin pipeline and low activity needs coaching on prospecting. A rep with thin pipeline and high activity needs coaching on qualification — they're working hard on things that aren't converting. The coverage analysis tells you who needs help. The activity data tells you what kind.
