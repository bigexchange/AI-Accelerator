Polish and Format a Presentation

Sharpen content, tighten structure, and calibrate messaging for any audience — from a CISO roundtable to a board deck. Covers narrative, proof points, audience reframing, and the review loop.

🎯
What you'll have
A presentation with a clear narrative arc, audience-calibrated messaging, and language that sounds like BigID wrote it — not like AI generated it.
⚡
Before you startGive Claude two things upfront: (1) context about BigID — paste a recent one-pager, press release, or your current messaging doc; (2) context about your audience — who's in the room, what they care about, what you want them to think when it's over.
1

Start with your audience

Before touching a slide, get this right. Everything else filters through it.

Audience framing I'm building a presentation about BigID for [audience]. Here's who they are: [2–3 sentences — role, what they care about, what they know about us]. What are the 3–5 things this audience most needs to understand, and the one thing I want them to walk away believing?
Example I'm building a presentation for Fortune 500 CISOs at a roundtable dinner. They're evaluating DSPM vendors, skeptical of pitches, and respond to specificity and peer evidence. What are the 3–5 things this audience most needs to understand, and the one thing I want them to walk away believing?
2

Build or audit the structure

Starting from scratch I'm building a [X]-slide presentation for [audience] about [topic]. Goal: [goal]. Help me create a clear narrative arc. For each slide give me: the title, the one thing it needs to communicate, and any supporting evidence it should include.
Auditing an existing deck Here's the structure of my BigID presentation [paste slide titles]. Audience: [describe]. Goal: [goal]. What's missing, redundant, in the wrong order, or where does the narrative break down?
3

Write the slide content

Single slide Write the content for a slide titled "[title]." Audience: [describe]. The one thing this slide needs to communicate is [key message]. 3–4 bullets. Each bullet is one clear, specific sentence — no fluff, no passive voice.
✓ Good

— Built for compliance audits, not AI data exposure
— No visibility into unstructured data or shadow AI usage
— Can't see what your models are training on

✗ Avoid

— "Organizations face many data security challenges"
— "BigID provides comprehensive data security solutions"
— "Leverage our robust platform to drive value"

💡
If Claude goes generic, push backSay: "These bullets are too vague. Make each one more specific. Replace any abstract claim with a concrete example, a number, or a named risk. Cut anything that could apply to any security vendor."
4

Calibrate tone by audience

Three-audience reframe Here's the core message: [paste]. Write three versions: — CISO: risk and control — CFO: cost and liability — Board: fiduciary responsibility and competitive risk For each, lead with what they care about most and translate any technical language into their frame of reference.
5

Polish the language

Language pass Review this slide copy [paste]. Edit so that: every bullet leads with a business implication, no sentence uses passive voice, no bullet starts with "BigID" or "Our," and nothing reads like AI-generated marketing copy. Be direct. Cut filler.
Three words to cut every time
Comprehensive→ say what it actually covers
Robust→ say what it actually does
Leverages→ use, power, or enable
6

Run the review loop

Structural review Read this full presentation [paste]. Does the narrative build clearly? Where does it lose momentum? What's the single weakest slide?
Devil's advocate You're a skeptical CISO in the room. Read this presentation and give me the 5 hardest objections you'd have. Then help me either address each one in the deck or prepare the presenter with responses.
Final pass Read this like you're the audience. What's the one thing that would make you sit up straighter? What's the one thing that would make you tune out? What question would you definitely ask at the end?

Pro tips

🎯
Negative constraints work"No generic statements," "no bullets that start with BigID," "nothing that could apply to any vendor" — these make Claude's output dramatically better.
🔀
Ask for three alternativesDon't just ask Claude to "try again." Tell it exactly what didn't work — too formal, too vague, wrong audience — then ask for three alternatives.
📑
One section at a timeGive Claude one section at a time to get detailed, useful feedback — not a surface-level skim of the whole deck at once.
