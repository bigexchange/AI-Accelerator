Build a Target Account List

A good target account list isn't a dump from Salesforce filtered by industry. It's a prioritized, researched set of accounts that match your ICP, show buying signals, and fit your territory — ranked by who's most likely to move.

📋
What you'll have
A scored, signal-rich, research-backed list of target accounts — built from scratch, checked against Salesforce coverage, and ready for outreach.

What this is

A good target account list isn't a dump from Salesforce filtered by industry. It's a prioritized, researched set of accounts that match your ICP, show buying signals, and fit your territory — ranked by who's most likely to move. Claude can help you build one from scratch, clean up an existing list, score a set of accounts against your criteria, or layer in intent and signal data you'd otherwise spend hours pulling manually.

What you need before you start

Depending on what you're building, connect some combination of these:

  • Salesforce — to check existing account coverage, open opportunities, and prior history
  • Web search — for live research on companies, funding rounds, news, and signals
  • LinkedIn (paste profiles or company pages directly) — for org structure, headcount, and hiring signals
  • HubSpot — if your marketing engagement data lives there
  • A CSV or spreadsheet — if you're scoring or cleaning an existing list

You don't need all of these. Even with just web search and a clear ICP definition, Claude will do a lot of the heavy lifting.

Step 1: Define your ICP before you build anything

If you give Claude a vague ICP, you get a vague list. Spend two minutes defining your ideal customer profile before you run any prompt. Claude can help you sharpen it if you're not sure.

Prompt to define or sharpen your ICP Help me define a tight ICP for my territory. I sell BigID — an AI data security and governance platform — to enterprise companies. My territory is [region / vertical / segment]. Based on what you know about BigID's ideal customer, help me define: company size (revenue or headcount), industry verticals, likely titles and personas, tech stack signals that indicate fit, and business triggers that suggest they're in a buying moment. Ask me questions if you need more context.

Example output you're looking for: A crisp ICP definition you can paste into every list-building prompt. Something like:

Target: Enterprise companies (2,500+ employees, $500M+ revenue) in financial services, healthcare, pharma, or tech. Key personas: CISO, Chief Data Officer, VP of Data Security, Head of AI Governance. Tech signals: cloud-heavy (AWS/Azure/GCP), Snowflake or Databricks users, recent AI/ML investment. Buying triggers: recent data breach or regulatory fine, AI initiative announcement, M&A activity, new CISO hire, SOC 2 / HIPAA / GDPR compliance pressure.

Save this. You'll use it in every prompt below.

Step 2: Build a net-new list from scratch

You have a territory. You need accounts. Start here.

I need to build a target account list for my sales territory. Here's my ICP: [paste your ICP definition]. My territory covers [region / vertical / named accounts]. Search for companies that match this profile. For each account, give me: company name, headquarters, estimated employee count, industry, why they fit my ICP, and any recent news or signals that suggest they might be in a buying moment right now. Aim for [X] accounts to start.
Example I need to build a target account list for my enterprise territory covering financial services companies in the Northeast US. ICP: 2,500+ employees, CISO or CDO as the likely buyer, cloud-heavy infrastructure, regulatory pressure around data privacy or AI. Search for companies that fit this profile. Give me 20 accounts with: name, HQ, size, why they fit, and any recent signals — funding, new security hire, breach news, AI initiative — that suggest active buying potential.

Step 3: Score and prioritize an existing list

You have a list — from a conference, a marketing campaign, a territory assignment, or a CSV from ops. Now you need to figure out who's worth calling first.

Here's a list of companies I've been given to work [paste list or upload CSV]. My ICP is: [paste]. Score each account from 1–3 based on ICP fit: 1 = strong fit, 2 = possible fit, 3 = unlikely fit. For each account, give me a one-line reason for the score. Then rank the full list by priority and flag the top 10 I should focus on first.
If you want more depth on the top accounts Take the top 10 accounts from the list above and do a quick research pass on each. For each one give me: a one-paragraph company overview, the most likely buyer title, any recent news that's relevant to a BigID conversation, and one specific angle I should use when I reach out.

Step 4: Layer in buying signals

A company that fits your ICP is a target. A company that fits your ICP and is showing active signals is a priority. Here's how to find the signal.

Funding or growth signals Search for [Company] news from the past 90 days. Did they announce new funding, a merger or acquisition, a major product launch, or significant headcount growth? Any of these could indicate budget availability or a buying window. Summarize what you find and flag whether it opens a BigID conversation.
Regulatory or breach signals Search for recent news about [Company] and data privacy, security incidents, regulatory actions, or compliance initiatives. Anything that suggests they're under pressure around data governance or AI risk is relevant. Give me a summary and a suggested angle for outreach.
AI initiative signals Search for any public announcements, job postings, or press coverage indicating that [Company] is investing in AI — new AI products, ML infrastructure, LLM deployments, or AI governance initiatives. This is a strong buying signal for BigID. Summarize what you find.
Hiring signals Search LinkedIn and job boards for recent security, data governance, or AI-related job postings at [Company]. A company hiring a Head of AI Governance or a Data Security Engineer is likely building infrastructure we can support. What are they hiring for right now?
Combined signal prompt Research [Company] for buying signals relevant to BigID. Look for: recent funding or M&A activity, AI or data infrastructure announcements, security or compliance news, new executive hires in security or data roles, and any regulatory pressure. Give me a signal summary and a recommended outreach angle based on what you find.

Step 5: Compare a list to your Salesforce coverage

Before you spend time prospecting an account, check if it's already in your CRM — worked by a colleague, in an active deal, or previously lost.

Here's a list of target accounts I'm considering adding to my outreach: [paste list]. Check Salesforce for each account. Tell me: which ones already exist as accounts, what the current status is (active opportunity, closed lost, no activity), who owns them, and which ones are clean and available for me to pursue.
Follow-up For the accounts that are already in Salesforce with no active opportunity and no activity in the past 180 days — pull the history and tell me if there's anything worth knowing before I reach out cold.

Step 6: Build the outreach-ready version

Once you have a scored, prioritized list, build the version you'll actually work from — with the context you need to act, not just a company name and a phone number.

Take my top 15 target accounts and build me a working prospecting sheet. For each account include: company name, primary target persona and likely title, best entry point (cold outreach / warm intro / event / marketing touch), one-sentence ICP fit rationale, one current signal worth referencing in outreach, and a suggested first line for a cold email or LinkedIn message. Format it so I can drop it into a spreadsheet.

Ongoing: keeping the list fresh

A target account list is a living document. Here's how to refresh it regularly without starting from scratch.

Weekly signal check Run a quick signal check on my top 20 target accounts. Search for news from the past 7 days on each. Flag any company where something has changed — new funding, executive hire, breach, AI announcement, or major partnership — that opens a new angle for outreach.
After an event or campaign Here's the registration list from [event] [paste or upload]. Cross-reference it against my target account list. Flag anyone who attended that I should prioritize for follow-up. For anyone new who isn't on my list, score them against my ICP and tell me if they're worth adding.
Quarterly refresh My target account list was last updated [date]. Here's the current list [paste]. Search for any significant changes at these companies in the past 90 days — leadership changes, funding, M&A, AI initiatives, or security news. Flag accounts where something has changed that warrants a fresh outreach, and suggest 5–10 new accounts I should add based on my ICP.

Tips for better lists

Specificity beats volume. Twenty well-researched, signal-rich accounts will outperform two hundred names with no context every time. Use Claude to go deeper on fewer accounts rather than wider on more.

Always anchor to a buying trigger. A company that fits your ICP but has no active trigger is a someday account. A company that fits your ICP and just hired a new CISO, announced an AI initiative, or disclosed a data incident is a call-this-week account. Build your list around triggers, not just firmographics.

Combine your tools. Salesforce tells you what's already been worked. Web search tells you what's happening now. LinkedIn tells you who's there and what they're focused on. The best lists use all three. Claude can synthesize across all of them in one prompt if you have the connectors enabled.

Save the ICP definition. The prompt you write to define your ICP in Step 1 is reusable. Save it somewhere accessible and paste it into every list-building prompt so you don't have to re-explain yourself every session.

Use Claude to write the first touch, too. Once you've built and researched your list, you already have everything Claude needs to draft a personalized first outreach for each account. Don't waste the research — ask for the email while you're in the same session.
